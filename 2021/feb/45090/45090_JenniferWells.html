TOP STORIES

The roots of slavery still visible today, says Harvard professor

A&S welcomed Harvard University Professor Vincent Brown to deliver a powerful lecture to hundreds of viewers as part of U of T’s Black History Month events. As guest speaker for the A&S Decanal Lecture on February 3, the award-winning author and historian connected today’s police violence against Black people to the brutal slave trade centuries ago.

“The violence of slavery has established a kind of continuous logic that I think we're still struggling with today,” Brown said.

Alumni offer career tips to hundreds of students, grads at Next Steps Conference

Go with your passion, take opportunities as they come and don’t underestimate the power of networking.

These are just some of the career tips alumni panelists shared with students and new grads at this year’s Next Steps Conference. Online for the first time, the conference featured dozens of A&S alumni panelists with a wide range of experiences, all eager to share their advice and perspectives.

School of Cities welcomes renowned urban planning expert

Urban planning and data science academic Karen Chapple joins the School of Cities as its new director.

Chapple comes from the University of California, Berkeley, where she is professor and chair of city and regional planning and holds the Carmel P. Friesen Chair in Urban Studies.

“I very much look forward to working with Karen as we collectively strive toward a brighter urban future,” says U of T President Meric Gertler.

Assistant professor receives 2020 Polanyi Prize in Physics

An assistant professor in the Department of Physics has been awarded the 2020 Polanyi Prize in Physics for her insights into dark matter and the nature of the universe.

The prestigious prize is awarded in honour of Ontario’s Nobel Prize winner John C. Polanyi, who won the 1986 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his research in chemical kinetics.

Miriam Diamond’s search for dark matter — which makes up about one quarter of the universe — could revolutionize the core of modern physics and bring about a better understanding of the universe.

HIGHLIGHTS

PhD candidate creates a digital group to promote diversity and accessibility

PhD candidate Sarah Qidwai has created a digital community focused on improving diversity and accessibility for academic discussions, conferences and events.

Within its initial months, the Virtual History of Science, Technology and Medicine group has grown to include hundreds of members from around the world and a full slate of planned events.

“It ended up snowballing into something much bigger than any of us who were involved could ever have imagined,” says Qidwai.

A&S alumnus helps document Toronto’s 1980s hardcore punk scene

History alumnus Simon Harvey put his degree to good use as a contributing author of an award-winning book about Toronto hardcore punk in the 1980s, Tomorrow is Too Late.

“It’s a natural fit for me to want to bring together history and punk rock, and I would absolutely say there was an influence from my U of T history studies. I think it was unavoidable,” says Harvey.

Watch the video: Simon Harvey reads the intro to Tomorrow is Too Late.

Diversity has advantage in tech, alumnus says

Alumnus Andre Charoo was recently featured on Rep Matters Perspective: The Advantage of Being Black and Canadian in Tech.

“You could be finding a unique way to service the majority of society at large, because your different view, because of your life experiences, because you are the only ‘x’ person in the room,” says Charoo, founder and managing partner of Maple VC and co-chair of C100.

Arts & Science has a long and impressive history of supporting student innovation. The Centre for Entrepreneurship is being revamped to provide exceptional mentorship, expertise, resources and strategic connections for all stages of innovation.

Alumna explores our interaction with ‘things’ in new religion course

A&S alumna Erin Vearncombe is exploring our human interaction with things through a new Department for the Study of Religion course, Museums and Material Religion.

“We are profoundly shaped by the things around us,” Vearncombe says. “We may think we’re above the world of things, but really, things influence us every day in ways of which we’re not even conscious.”

First-year math student creates prize-winning software

First-year math student Euan Pound hopes to help improve access to mental health resources for remote communities through an innovation created by his Superior Innovations Team.

Mental health impacts quality of life and education, especially for Indigenous students in northern communities, says Pound, whose team captured the $10,000 top prize at the Convergence.Tech Digital Inclusion Challenge 2020.

“We came up with a solution that could fix the problem of low bandwidth in northern communities and provide an alternative for camera-shy clients, while still allowing the caregiver to see expressions and non-verbal cues through the avatar,” he explains.

Urban studies students connect with seniors online during COVID-19

An oral history research project is connecting students with isolated seniors via Skype, thanks to an urban studies course taught by Aditi Mehta, an assistant professor, teaching stream, and American journalist Alexa Mills.

“In urban studies, you learn about processes such as suburbanization or the construction of highways,” Mehta says. “It was really cool to see the students hear about people’s lived experiences of those historical processes, which doesn’t normally happen."

 
 

THE IMPACT OF GIVING

Professor emeritus leaves his stamp on Earth Sciences with field trip fund

Department of Earth Sciences Professor Emeritus Henry Halls recently sold part of his decades-old stamp collection to establish the Henry C. Halls Graduate Field Trip Fund, which will support new and stimulating field geological learning experiences for graduate students in Canada and beyond.

Halls, who started the collection as an eight-year-old some 70 years ago, is pleased to give back to U of T.

“Hopefully students will gain the excitement, knowledge, experiences and memories that I have treasured over my own life of adventure.”

Life lessons inspire alumna to create scholarship in mother’s name

Alumna Alethea Lyn is honouring the life lessons she learned from her late mother with a U of T scholarship in her name, the Gloria Lyn Memorial Fund.

“My mother used to say that, as long as you had an education, you would always be able to take care of yourself,” says Alethea.

Her mother had started school in a village post office in Jamaica and went on to earn a master of arts in English literature in 1971 from U of T, thanks to a scholarship.
