DECEMBER 2020
 
 
MESSAGE FROM THE CHAIR
 
 
Dear Alumni and Community Supporters,

This edition of Politics resembles a series of snapshots taken of the same subject — the Department of Political Science — over time. One shot focuses on the ways in which faculty, staff and students alike have risen to the current challenge of delivering almost all courses online. This includes a feature on two sparkling courses that centre on the politics of pandemics. A second frame is more retrospective; it catches up with two graduates of the Department, both working in healthcare, who demonstrate the versatility of an education in political science. The third shot points to the future and foregrounds sensational undergraduates off to graduate school, recently minted PhDs now teaching the next generation of students at universities around the world and postdoctoral fellows breaking trail on future directions in political science research.

What unites all three snapshots is the curiosity, creativity and commitment of our faculty, staff and students. Of course, moving from curiosity to knowledge is not cheap. My colleagues, as you will see in this newsletter, have been adept at generating research funding from public sources (like SSHRC); our students have competed successfully for publicly funded scholarships (like the Ontario Graduate Scholarship); and both faculty and students have attracted support from philanthropic sources that bear well-known names, including Fulbright, Killam, Vanier and Ignatieff. Still, many of our students and faculty depend on the Department financially to ignite and sustain their creative spark. Alumnus Avram Denburg recalls his life-changing participation as an undergraduate student in the first incarnation of Ron Deibert’s Citizen Lab. The Lab is now justly renowned for its work exposing cyber threats to citizens and individuals, and Ron’s new book, "Reset", formed the basis of this year’s Massey Lectures. Yet in the beginning, the Citizen Lab was just a great idea, and Ron had to cobble together funding from several sources to launch it. I’m proud to say that the Department provided modest seed funding at the time, surely the best bet we’ve ever made.

With your help we will continue to support our students and faculty, believing that their curiosity, creativity and commitment to addressing the big and pressing problems that face us — whether cyber espionage, climate change, racism, homelessness, or many others — will benefit us all. So please, if you are able, help us help them by making an online donation to the Department of Political Science. You may choose to give either to the Political Science Opportunity Fund or to one of the named scholarships. Every little bit helps!

With many thanks,

Rob Vipond
Professor and Acting Chair
